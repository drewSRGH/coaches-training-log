Butter Wheel — Flattop Toasting
  • 1
    Load at Opening

    Fill with softened butter. Position on the warm edge of the flattop — pliable, not pooling.

  • 2
    Roll, Don't Press

    Drag the cut face of bun or bread lightly across the wheel. Then place butter-side down on medium-heat zone.

  • 3
    Watch for Golden Color

    Pale = not enough time. Dark = too much heat. Adjust zone position as needed.

  • 4
    Refill During Service

    Dry wheel drags and tears the bread. Refill before it runs out.

  • 5
    End of Day

    Discard remaining butter — never save. Disassemble, wash, rinse, and fully dry before storing.

2. Fire Safety & Emergency Response
🔥 Know before you start your shift: Where is the nearest Class K fire extinguisher? Where is the fire suppression pull station? Where are the emergency exits? If you don't know — ask before you cook.
Fire ClassTypeExtinguisher

Electrical firesCO2 or dry chemical — NEVER water
🚨 NEVER use water on a grease or oil fire. Water causes a violent steam explosion that spreads burning oil and causes severe burns. Class K extinguisher only.
Grease Fire Response — In Order
  • 1
    Turn Off the Heat Source

    Shut off the burner, fryer, or oven immediately. Remove the fuel source.

  • 2
    Do NOT Open the Oven Door

    If fire is inside the oven, keep the door closed — it will smother. Opening it feeds oxygen to the fire.

  • 3
    Smother if Possible

    A lid or sheet pan can smother a small pan fire. Slide it over carefully — do not drop it.

  • 4
    Use Class K Extinguisher

    Aim at the base of the flame. Sweep side to side. Stand back — extinguisher discharge is forceful.

  • 5
    Evacuate & Call 911

    If the fire spreads beyond the immediate station, activate the fire alarm, evacuate all staff and guests, and call 911 from outside the building.

Fire Prevention — Daily Habits
Keep combustibles (towels, paper, cardboard) 12"+ away from all open flames and heating elements
Never leave fryer, chargriller, or oven unattended during service
Empty fryer and chargriller drip trays regularly — overfull trays ignite
Clean grease buildup from oven exterior, hood filters, and exhaust daily
Turn off all equipment at the end of every shift — verify before leaving
Report any malfunctioning equipment immediately — do not operate broken equipment

Burn Treatment — Minor Burns
  • 1
    Cool the Burn Immediately

    Run cool (not ice cold) water over the burned area for 10–20 minutes. Do not use ice — ice damages tissue and worsens the burn.

  • 2
    Do Not Pop Blisters

    Blisters are the body's natural protection. Popping them increases infection risk significantly.

  • 3
    Cover Loosely

    Apply a sterile non-stick dressing from the first aid kit. Do not wrap tightly.

  • 4
    Report All Burns

    All burns must be reported to the manager on duty, no matter how minor. Document in the incident log.

  • 5
    Seek Medical Attention For

    Burns larger than 3 inches, burns on hands, face, or feet, burns that blister immediately, or any burn with white or charred skin — these require emergency care.

Temperature Danger Zone
41°F – 135°F = DANGER ZONE
Bacteria multiply rapidly in this range. Food left in the danger zone for more than 2 cumulative hours must be discarded — no exceptions, no matter the cost.

The 2-hour rule applies to: food left on counters between service, hot well items that drop below 140°F, cold prep items left out during prep, and any food in transit between stations.
⚠️ When in doubt, throw it out. Never taste food to determine if it is safe. Bacteria that cause illness are odorless and tasteless.
Cross-Contamination Prevention
  • 1
    Separate Raw & Ready-to-Eat Foods

    Raw chicken, beef, and seafood must never contact cooked food, produce, or ready-to-eat items. Use dedicated cutting boards, containers, and utensils for each.

  • 2
    Color-Coded Cutting Boards

    Red = raw meat, Yellow = raw poultry, Blue = raw seafood, Green = produce, White = ready-to-eat. Never swap or substitute. Replace boards with deep knife marks immediately.

  • 3
    Sanitize Between Tasks

    Wash, rinse, and sanitize all surfaces, utensils, and cutting boards when switching between raw proteins or between raw and cooked food. Do not wipe with a dry towel — use sanitizer solution.

  • 4
    Dedicated Tongs & Utensils

    Separate tongs for raw and cooked protein at all stations. Do not use the same spatula on raw burger patties that you use to plate a finished burger.

  • 5
    Proper Storage Order in Walk-In

    Top shelf: ready-to-eat and cooked foods. Middle: raw beef and pork. Bottom: raw poultry. Raw seafood in sealed containers. This prevents drip contamination.

Food Labeling & FIFO
  • 1
    Label Everything

    All food in storage must be labeled with the item name and the date it was prepared or opened. No unlabeled food in walk-in, cooler, or prep area — ever.

  • 2
    FIFO — First In, First Out

    Always use older product before newer product. When stocking deliveries, rotate new stock to the back and move older stock to the front. Check dates daily.

  • 3
    7-Day Rule

    Most prepared foods must be used or discarded within 7 days of preparation. Check with your manager for specific hold times on individual items.

  • 4
    Never Top Off

    Do not add fresh product on top of old product in hot wells, prep containers, or on the line. Always empty, clean, and refill.

☐  After handling raw meat, poultry, or seafood

6. Chemical Safety & Cleaning Standards
  • 1
    Read Labels Before Use

    Never use a cleaning chemical without reading the label first. Know the dilution ratio, required PPE, and proper application method for every chemical you use.

  • 2
    Never Mix Chemicals

    Mixing cleaning chemicals — particularly bleach with ammonia or acid-based cleaners — creates toxic gases. Always use one product at a time and rinse thoroughly between applications.

  • 3
    Label All Chemical Containers

    Never transfer chemicals to unlabeled spray bottles. All chemical containers must be labeled with the product name and dilution ratio at all times.

  • 4
    Store Chemicals Separately

    All cleaning chemicals must be stored below and away from food, food contact surfaces, and food storage areas. Chemicals and food must never share a shelf.

  • 5
    Sanitizer Concentration

    Sanitizer solution must be tested with test strips before each service period. Concentration must be within the correct range — too weak doesn't sanitize, too strong is a hazard. Log test results.

3-Compartment Sink Procedure
  • 1
    Wash

    Hot water (110°F+) with dish soap. Scrub all food residue from surfaces.

  • 2
    Rinse

    Clean warm water. Remove all soap and food residue completely.

  • 3
    Sanitize

    Approved sanitizer solution at correct concentration. Submerge or coat all surfaces. Allow to air dry — do not towel dry sanitized items.

ℹ️ SDS (Safety Data Sheets) for all chemicals used in this kitchen are kept on file. You have the right to access these at any time. Ask your manager for the SDS binder location.
7. Slip, Trip & Fall Prevention
  • 1
    Clean Spills Immediately

    Any liquid spill on the floor is a slip hazard. Stop what you're doing and clean it up or place a wet floor sign before continuing other tasks.

  • 2
    Use Wet Floor Signs

    Place wet floor signs any time you mop or clean the floor. Leave them up until the floor is completely dry.

  • 3
    Keep Walkways Clear

    No boxes, equipment, or obstacles in kitchen walkways or near emergency exits at any time.

  • 4
    Walk — Never Run

    In a kitchen environment, running causes collisions with hot equipment and other staff carrying hot food. Walk at all times.

  • 5
    Announce Hot Items in Transit

    Always call "behind" or "hot behind" when carrying hot food or equipment behind another person. Call "corner" when approaching a blind corner.

  • 6
    Anti-Fatigue Mats

    Use anti-fatigue mats at your station. Check that mats lay flat with no curled edges — a curled mat is a trip hazard.

  • 7
    Carry Loads Safely

    Bend at the knees when lifting heavy items. Never lift more than you can safely carry. Use a cart for large or heavy loads. Ask for help — lifting injuries are serious.

  • 8
    Grease Trap & Floor Drain Maintenance

    Keep floor drains clear and grease traps emptied regularly. Overflowing drains and grease on floors are both slip hazards and health code violations.

8. Knife & Equipment Safety
Knife Safety Rules
  • 1
    A Sharp Knife is a Safe Knife

    Dull knives require more force and are more likely to slip. Keep knives sharp and honed. Report dull knives to a manager.

  • 2
    Cut Away from Your Body

    Always direct the blade away from yourself and others. Curl your fingertips under in a "bear claw" grip to protect your fingers.

  • 3
    Never Leave Knives in a Sink

    A knife in soapy water is invisible and dangerous. Wash knives separately, immediately, and store them in a knife block or magnetic strip.

  • 4
    Carry Knives Point Down

    When transporting a knife, hold it at your side with the blade pointing down and edge facing back. Announce your movement.

  • 5
    If a Knife Falls — Let It Go

    Never try to catch a falling knife. Step back and let it fall. A cut hand is worse than a blade on the floor.

Equipment Safety Rules
  • 1
    Never Operate Unfamiliar Equipment

    Do not operate any piece of equipment you have not been trained on. Ask your trainer or manager before using any new equipment.

  • 2
    Machine Guards Must Be in Place

    Never operate slicers, mixers, or other equipment with guards removed. Guards are there to prevent serious injury.

  • 3
    Lockout / Tagout

    Before cleaning any equipment with moving parts (slicer, mixer, meat grinder), turn it off, unplug it, and confirm it cannot be turned on before placing hands near blades or moving parts.

  • 4
    Report Damaged Equipment Immediately

    Frayed cords, cracked handles, loose guards, or malfunctioning switches must be reported immediately. Tag the equipment "Out of Service" and do not use it until repaired.

9. Incident Reporting
📋 All workplace injuries, near-misses, and unsafe conditions must be reported immediately to the manager on duty — no matter how minor they seem. Early reporting protects you, your coworkers, and the business.
What Must Be Reported
Any burn, cut, laceration, or injury — no matter how minor
Any slip, trip, or fall — even if no injury occurred
Any near-miss (close call that could have caused injury)
Any malfunctioning or damaged equipment
Any unsafe condition observed in the kitchen
Any food safety concern (temperature violation, contamination)
Any chemical spill or exposure
After an Injury — Steps
  • 1
    Ensure Safety First

    Move away from hazard. Secure the area if needed to protect others.

  • 2
    Provide First Aid

    Use the first aid kit for minor injuries. Call 911 for serious injuries. Do not delay emergency care to complete paperwork.

  • 3
    Report to Manager on Duty

    Notify your manager immediately — verbally, then in writing via the incident report form.

  • 4
    Complete Incident Report

    Document what happened, when, where, who was involved, and what caused it. Be specific. Your manager will retain this on file.
